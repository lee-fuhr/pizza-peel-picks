How to choose a pizza peel

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Choose a pizza peel based on your oven type, dough skill, and budget: wooden peels are best for sliding raw dough into a hot oven, while thin metal peels excel at retrieving baked pizzas. For high-temp ovens (500°F+), a long-handled metal peel is essential, but a medium wood peel is more forgiving for beginners. Prioritize a peel that lets you launch with confidence, size and handle length matter as much as material.

What material should your pizza peel be?

The three main peel materials are wood, metal, and composite. Wood (usually birch or maple) is the classic choice for launching: its porous surface grips a dusting of flour or cornmeal, helping raw dough slide off smoothly onto a hot stone or steel. Wood doesn’t conduct heat, so it won’t burn your hands if it touches the oven deck briefly. However, it’s thicker and heavier, and it can warp or crack if soaked or left in a hot oven. Metal peels are typically thin aluminum or stainless steel, sometimes with perforations. They slide under a baked pizza effortlessly and are indispensable for turning or retrieving pies. The thin edge makes them tricky for launching raw dough, the dough can stick unless you use plenty of flour or a special mesh screen. Many home cooks keep both: a wood peel for launching and a metal peel for turning and retrieval. Composite peels (like nylon or fiberglass blends) sit between wood and metal in price and performance. They’re lightweight, non-stick, and more durable than wood, but they aren’t as heat-proof as metal for very high temperatures. For most home ovens, a quality composite can work well for both launching and retrieving, especially if you prefer a single-peel setup.

Round or square peel: which shape is better?

A round peel matches the shape of your pizza and looks classic, but a square peel offers more surface area for larger pies and easier maneuvering on a wide oven rack. Round peels typically range from 12 to 16 inches in diameter; square peels often measure 14x14 or 16x16 inches. If you bake 12-inch Neapolitan-style pies, a round 14-inch peel gives you a few inches of clearance. For American-style rectangular pizzas or multiple small pies, a square peel lets you launch two at once. The real deciding factor is your oven opening. A 16-inch round peel won’t fit through a standard 15-inch-wide home oven door at an angle. Measure the width of your oven rack’s usable space and the height of the door opening before choosing. Square peels can be especially helpful when you need to slide a pizza onto a baking steel that takes up the whole rack.

What size pizza peel do you need?

The peel size should match your pizza size plus a few inches of safe handling room. If you mostly bake 12-inch pizzas, a 14-inch peel gives you a 1-inch border all around, enough to slide under the dough without tearing the edge. For 14-inch pies, step up to a 16-inch peel. Remember that bigger peels are heavier and can feel clumsy, especially with a long handle. Also consider your oven. A peel’s handle length needs to clear the hot oven walls, but a very long handle (40+ inches) is unwieldy for home use unless you have a deep outdoor oven. For a standard home oven, 20 to 30 inches of total length is comfortable. If you use a pizza steel that sits low in the oven, a shorter handle (16–20 inches) may be safer because you have less leverage to tip the pizza.

How thick should a pizza peel be?

Thickness affects both launch performance and retrieval ease. Wood peels are typically 1/4 to 3/8 inch thick. Thinner wood peels (around 1/4 inch) slide under dough more easily but can flex or crack under heavy use. Thicker wood peels are sturdier but make it harder to slide beneath a baked pizza because the edge is too blunt. For launching, a slightly flexible, thin steel peel (about 1/16 inch) is ideal because it shimmies under the crust. For retrieval, a thin rigid steel peel (1/16 to 1/8 inch) works best. A good compromise is a two-peel system: a thin wood or composite peel for launching (around 1/4 inch or less) and a thin metal peel for retrieval. If you buy only one all-purpose peel, choose a medium-thickness option (about 1/8 inch for metal, 5/16 inch for wood) that is strong enough to hold the pizza but thin enough to get under it.

Do you need a perforated pizza peel?

Perforated (slotted) metal peels have small holes or slits cut into the blade. These holes allow excess flour or cornmeal to fall through when you shake the peel before launching, reducing the risk of a burnt, gritty crust. They also let heat circulate when you retrieve the pizza, which can keep the bottom from getting soggy if you’re holding the pie for a moment before serving. However, perforations can allow small pieces of dough or toppings to fall through, and they can catch on sticky dough if you haven’t dusted well enough. For beginners, a solid wood or composite peel is more forgiving because the surface holds the dusting better. Perforated peels are best for experienced users who want a clean launch and quick retrieval, especially in high-heat ovens where excess flour would scorch.

How to launch a pizza from a peel without sticking

The key to a non-stick launch is a well-dusted peel and a quick, confident motion. Start by sprinkling a generous layer of semolina, cornmeal, or coarse flour on the peel. Place the stretched dough on top and give the peel a gentle shake, the dough should slide freely. If it doesn’t, lift the stuck spot and add more dusting underneath. Work quickly: raw dough sitting on the peel will start to absorb moisture and stick. Shape the pizza on a separate floured surface or counter, then transfer it to the peel. Add toppings sparingly, heavy wet toppings increase the risk of sticking. When you’re ready to launch, set the peel edge at the back of the hot stone or steel, then jerk the peel back sharply with a swift horizontal pull. Don’t try to push the pizza off; the sharp stop dislodges it cleanly. Practice on a cool oven several times until the motion feels natural.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a pizza peel in a wood-fired oven?

Yes, but you’ll need a long-handled peel to keep your hands away from the intense heat (700–900°F). Metal peels are standard for wood-fired ovens because they won’t char or burn. Some bakers use a wooden launching peel and a long metal turning peel for rotating the pizza during baking.

What’s the difference between a pizza peel and a pizza screen?

A pizza peel is a flat, shovel-like tool for transferring pizzas in and out of the oven. A pizza screen is a perforated metal disc that the pizza sits on during baking; it’s used to prevent sticking and create a crisper bottom, but it’s not a peel itself. You can place the screen on a peel to launch, then remove the screen mid-bake.

How do I clean and maintain a wood pizza peel?

Never soak a wood peel in water or put it in the dishwasher. After use, wipe it clean with a dry or slightly damp cloth, then let it air dry completely. Occasionally rub it with food-grade mineral oil to prevent cracking and preserve the surface. Avoid olive or vegetable oils, which can go rancid.

Is a pizza peel necessary for making pizza at home?

Technically no, you can build a pizza on a piece of parchment paper and slide that onto your stone or steel. But a peel makes the process much smoother, more controlled, and faster, especially if you bake multiple pizzas. It also helps you retrieve the finished pie without burning your fingers.

Can I use a metal peel for launching raw dough?

It’s possible, but harder. Metal peels have less friction than wood, so the dough slides more easily when properly dusted. However, if the dough sticks even a little, it’s difficult to free without tearing. Many home cooks use a wooden or composite peel for launching and reserve the metal peel for retrieval and turning.

What handle length is best for a home oven?

For a standard 30-inch-wide oven, a total peel length of about 24 to 30 inches gives you enough reach to place a pizza on the stone without your hand getting too close to the hot oven walls. Very long handles (40+ inches) are designed for deep commercial or outdoor ovens and can be awkward in a home kitchen.

In shortA good pizza peel is one you feel confident using, it matches the size of your pizzas, fits your oven, and suits your technique. Start with a mid-sized wood peel for launching and a small metal peel for retrieval if your budget allows; that combo covers nearly every home-pizza scenario. No single peel is perfect for everything, but the right choices turn a tricky slide into a satisfying launch every time.
