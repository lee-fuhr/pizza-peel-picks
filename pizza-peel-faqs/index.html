Pizza peel questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A pizza peel is essentially a flat paddle used to slide pizzas in and out of a hot oven. The best choice depends on your oven type, cooking style, and budget, with wood offering good slide and heat resistance, metal providing thinness for tight spaces, and composite balancing both. This guide answers the most common questions so you can pick the right tool and launch like a pro.

What size pizza peel should I buy?

Go a little bigger than the pizzas you plan to make. A common home-kitchen choice is around 14 inches wide, which easily handles a 12-inch pizza with some margin. If you bake smaller 10-inch pies, a 12-inch peel works well, but squeezing a 14-inch pizza onto a 12-inch peel is nearly impossible. For outdoor pizza ovens that get very hot and require a quick launch, a slightly wider peel (15 or 16 inches) gives you more stability. Just make sure the peel fits through your oven opening. Measure the depth of your oven&#8217;s mouth and the width of your stone or steel &#8212; the peel should be narrower than both so you can maneuver without scraping the sides.

Wood vs metal vs composite peels, which is best?

Each material has a clear trade-off. Wood peels are traditional, excellent for building and sliding raw dough because the porous surface grabs a bit of semolina or flour, reducing stickiness. However, they are thicker and can be heavy; they also need occasional oiling and should not be left wet. Thin metal peels (often aluminum or stainless steel) excel at sliding under a fully baked pizza to retrieve it. Their thin edge slides easily into tight gaps, and they handle high heat without damage. But they can be slippery for raw dough if not dusted generously. Composite peels attempt to merge the best of both &#8212; a thin edge with a non-porous, texturized surface &#8212; and are gaining popularity for home use. For most home bakers who cook on a stone or steel, a good metal peel plus a separate wooden build peel is the ideal combo.

How do I launch a pizza without sticking?

Prep is everything. Dust the peel lightly with flour, semolina, or cornmeal before placing the stretched dough on it. Once the dough is on the peel, do not let it sit more than a minute or two &#8212; the moisture in the dough can soak through, creating glue. Give the peel a gentle shake every few seconds to keep the dough moving. When you are ready to launch, tilt the peel at roughly a 20-degree angle over the preheated stone or steel, then jerk the peel back quickly toward you in one confident motion. A hesitant, slow push nearly always leads to a stuck, folded mess. If you use a wooden peel, a light dusting of rice flour is even more non-stick than standard wheat flour.

Do I need a separate turning peel?

If you own a pizza oven that reaches very high heat (like many wood-fired or propane ovens), a turning peel is invaluable. These are small, thin, often tapered metal peels designed to rotate the pizza mid-bake for even browning without pulling the pie out. They are also useful for shifting a stuck pizza. For a standard home oven that maxes out at 550°F, a regular peel can usually handle both launch and retrieval with a little practice. But if you frequently bake at high heat or want the precision, a dedicated turning peel is a worthwhile investment. Many home bakers end up using a long, thin metal peel for both tasks.

How do I care for and clean my pizza peel?

Wood and bamboo peels should never go in a dishwasher or sit submerged. Wipe them clean with a damp cloth and a little mild soap, then dry them immediately. To prevent cracking, apply a food-safe mineral oil or beeswax blend every few months. Avoid vegetable or olive oil, which can turn rancid. Metal peels are far more forgiving. Wash them with warm soapy water and dry them thoroughly to prevent water spots (especially on aluminum). Perforated metal peels need extra rinsing to clean out the holes. Composites can usually be wiped down like metal, but check the manufacturer&#8217;s guidance. Store all peels in a dry spot, hanging or flat, never stacked under heavy pots.

What is a pizza screen and do I need one?

A pizza screen is a round, flat mesh tray that you place dough on before baking. It allows heat to circulate underneath, crisping the crust without needing to launch directly onto a stone. Screens are great for beginners because they virtually eliminate sticking, and they are widely used in pizzerias for high-volume baking. However, screens prevent direct contact with the stone, so the bottom crust will be less charred and crunchy compared to a direct launch. Many home bakers use a screen for the first few minutes, then slide the pizza off onto the stone for final browning. If your oven&#8217;s top heat is weak, a screen can be a crutch; for a serious Neapolitan-style pie, skip it and master a wooden peel.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a baking sheet instead of a pizza peel?

You can use an inverted baking sheet as a makeshift peel in a pinch&#8212;especially a thin, rimless one. But a proper peel is thinner and longer, giving you better control to slide under a pizza and pull it out without burning your wrist. For regular pizza making, a real peel is worth the small investment.

How do I prevent the dough from sticking to the peel?

Dust the peel generously with fine semolina, rice flour, or cornmeal before placing the dough. Shake the peel every few seconds while topping the pizza, and launch quickly&#8212;dough left sitting will absorb moisture and cling. If it sticks, lift the edge and blow air under it or use a bench scraper to break the seal.

What is the difference between a launching peel and a turning peel?

A launching peel is the standard large, flat paddle used to place a raw pizza onto the stone and retrieve it at the end. A turning peel is smaller, narrower, and often has a tapered, angled edge designed to rotate the pizza mid-bake without lifting it off the stone. Many home bakers use a single metal peel for both jobs.

Should I oil my wooden peel?

Yes, but only with a food-grade mineral oil or beeswax blend, not cooking oils. Oiling prevents the wood from drying, cracking, or absorbing moisture and odors. Apply a light coat when the peel looks dry, let it soak in overnight, and wipe off any excess before use.

How long should a pizza peel handle be?

The handle should keep your hand far enough from the oven heat that you never need to get close to the opening. For a standard home oven, a handle around 8 to 12 inches works well. For outdoor high-heat ovens, look for handles 16 inches or longer to give you safe clearance.

Do I need a perforated peel?

Perforated peels have holes or slots that let excess flour fall away, reducing the chance of burnt flour spots on your pizza. They are excellent for launching on a stone because the dough slides more easily. The trade-off is that they can be weaker and harder to clean. They are not necessary, but many experienced bakers love them.

In shortPick a peel that matches your oven and skill level, and remember that technique matters more than gear. A well-dusted, confident launch on a basic wood peel beats an expensive composite peel used hesitantly every time. Start with a mid-size wood peel and a thin metal one for retrieval, and you will have the essentials covered without overspending.
